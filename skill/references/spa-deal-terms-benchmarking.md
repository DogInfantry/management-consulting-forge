# SPA Deal-Terms Benchmarking — What's Market, What's Aggressive, and How It Varies

> **When to read this.** You are negotiating, reviewing, or advising on the risk-allocation terms of a private acquisition agreement (SPA/APA), or you need to judge whether a proposed term is "market." This is the negotiation layer that sits on top of execution mechanics (`cross-border-ma-execution.md`) and diligence (`due-diligence-deep-dive.md`). It turns the purchase agreement from a black box into a set of *negotiable risk dials* you can benchmark and prioritise.

A private M&A agreement is, at heart, a **risk-allocation instrument**. Price sets who pays what; the deal terms set *who bears the risk that the target isn't what the seller said it was.* The best negotiators do not argue every clause — they know which three or four dials actually move money and reputation, know the market range for each, and spend their capital there. This file is the map of those dials, the market defaults, and how the defaults shift with deal dynamics (auction vs. bilateral, PE vs. strategic, buyer's vs. seller's market, jurisdiction, and whether warranty insurance is in play).

> **How to use the "market" labels.** Throughout, "common," "market," and "aggressive" describe *typical* mid-market private-deal practice as a starting reference, not a rule. Market practice shifts by year, deal size, sector, jurisdiction, and leverage. Use these as a *calibrated prior* to be updated by the specific deal — never as a substitute for current local counsel.

---

## 1. The Deal-Terms Anatomy — Six Dials That Allocate Risk

Every private acquisition agreement allocates risk through six mechanisms. Master these and you can read any SPA in the world.

1. **Representations & Warranties (R&W)** — the seller's statements of fact about the target. Breach → a claim.
2. **Disclosure** — what the seller carves out of the warranties (the disclosure letter/schedules). Disclosed = not a claim.
3. **Covenants** — promises about conduct (pre-closing ordinary-course operation; post-closing non-compete/non-solicit).
4. **Conditions** — what must be true to be *obliged* to close (see `cross-border-ma-execution.md` §3.4).
5. **Indemnities & limitations** — the money remedy for breach, and the caps, baskets, thresholds, and survival periods that bound it.
6. **Price mechanics** — how the headline number becomes cash: completion accounts vs. locked box, escrows, earn-outs.

The negotiation is a system: a buyer who concedes a low liability cap will fight harder for a longer survival period and a specific indemnity; a seller who accepts broad warranties will insist on tight disclosure and a de minimis. Trade across the system, not clause by clause.

## 2. Representations & Warranties — Scope and Qualification

### 2.1 What warranties do

Warranties serve two functions: **risk allocation** (a breach gives the buyer a damages/indemnity claim) and **diligence flushing** (to give accurate disclosure against a warranty, the seller must surface issues it might otherwise stay quiet about). A tight warranty set is often the buyer's most efficient diligence tool.

Core warranty categories: **title and capacity** (the seller owns the shares and can sell them), **capitalisation** (the share capital is as stated), **accounts** (the financials are true/fairly presented), **no undisclosed liabilities**, **material contracts**, **litigation**, **compliance/licences**, **tax**, **employment and pensions**, **IP and IT**, **real property**, **environmental**, and **anti-bribery/sanctions**. The **"fundamental" (or "key") warranties** — typically title, capacity, and capitalisation — are treated differently from "business" warranties throughout the limitation regime (higher or uncapped, longer survival).

### 2.2 The qualifiers — where the real negotiation happens

Two qualifier types shrink the seller's exposure and are fought over on every deal:

**Knowledge qualifiers ("so far as the seller is aware").** A warranty qualified by knowledge is only breached if the stated fact is both untrue *and* within the seller's knowledge. The negotiation is over:
- *Whose* knowledge — a named list of senior individuals is increasingly the market norm, rather than the entire organisation.
- *What standard* — **actual knowledge** (what they in fact knew) vs. **constructive knowledge** (what they *should* have known after "due and careful enquiry"). Buyers push for constructive knowledge with a duty of enquiry; sellers push for actual knowledge of a short named list. Market has drifted toward *actual knowledge of named individuals after reasonable enquiry.*

**Materiality qualifiers.** Warranties qualified by materiality ("no material breach," "no litigation involving more than [$X]") exclude trivial issues. Materiality is best **quantified by a specific monetary amount** rather than left to the abstract word — a defined threshold removes a whole category of disputes. Watch for **double materiality**: a warranty that is both materiality-qualified *and* whose breach must be "material" to be claimable, plus a materiality scrape (below) interacting with it. Double materiality is generally *seller-favourable and uncommon* in balanced deals; buyers resist it.

**The materiality scrape.** A buyer-favourable device that reads materiality qualifiers *out* of the warranties for the purpose of (a) determining whether a breach occurred and/or (b) calculating loss. Common in US-style deals, especially where the buyer is not insured; less common where W&I insurance sets the terms.

### 2.3 "No misleading omission" and disclosure of the data room

- A warranty that **no information given is materially misleading and nothing material has been omitted** is a strong buyer protection and reasonably common; sellers resist its breadth.
- **Disclosure of the entire data room** (i.e., everything uploaded is deemed disclosed against the warranties) is *seller-favourable* and its acceptability varies by market — some jurisdictions treat general data-room disclosure as standard, others require *specific* disclosure against specific warranties for it to bite. This single point can swing large amounts of risk; pin it down.

### 2.4 Repetition and bring-down

On deals with a signing-to-closing gap, the buyer wants the warranties to be **true at closing, not just at signing.** Mechanisms:
- **Repetition at completion** — the warranties are repeated at closing; a breach at that point may trigger a closing condition or a claim. Repetition at completion is common.
- **Bring-down certificate** — the seller certifies at closing that the warranties remain true. Less universally required than repetition, and heavily negotiated because it converts warranty accuracy into a closing condition (a walk-away right).
- **Standard:** whether accuracy is tested as "true in all material respects" or against a **Material Adverse Effect** standard determines how easily the buyer can refuse to close. The MAE standard is far more seller-protective (only a serious, durationally-significant deterioration lets the buyer walk).

## 3. Indemnities and the Limitation Architecture — Where the Money Is

This is the heart of the agreement and where a consultant's quantitative instinct pays off. The limitation package answers: *if the target isn't what was promised, how much can the buyer actually recover, for how long, and subject to what floors and ceilings?*

### 3.1 The liability cap

The maximum the seller can be required to pay. Benchmarks (business warranties, uninsured mid-market deal, as a % of enterprise/equity value):
- **Fundamental warranties (title, capacity, cap table):** frequently **100% of purchase price** (or uncapped) — the buyer must get the thing it paid for.
- **Business warranties:** a *fraction* of price. Ranges commonly seen span roughly **10%–50%** of consideration in bilateral deals; **competitive auctions and W&I-backed deals push caps far lower** — sometimes to a nominal amount (e.g., **$1** or 1% of price) with the buyer looking to insurance for real recovery.
- **Tax / specific indemnities:** often carved out of the general cap with their own (higher) caps or uncapped, because they cover known, quantified risks.
- **Fraud:** universally carved out — no cap, no time limit, disclosure no defence.

*What the cap attaches to* matters as much as its size: does the cap apply to the **whole agreement** (all claims, including covenants) or **just the warranties**? Sellers want an entire-agreement cap; buyers want covenants and specific indemnities to sit outside it.

### 3.2 Baskets, thresholds, and de minimis — the floors

Buyers can't chase every trivial breach; sellers want a buffer for the diligence-priced "known unknowns." Two floors:
- **De minimis (per-claim floor):** individual claims below a small threshold are ignored entirely and don't even count toward the basket. Common. Typically set as a small percentage of deal value.
- **Basket / threshold (aggregate floor):** claims must accumulate past an aggregate figure before the buyer can recover. Two flavours, and the difference is real money:
  - **Deductible ("excess") basket:** the buyer recovers only the amount *above* the threshold (like an insurance deductible). Seller-favourable.
  - **Tipping ("first-dollar") basket:** once claims cross the threshold, the buyer recovers *from the first dollar*. Buyer-favourable.
  - Both are common; the deductible/tipping choice and the threshold size (often ~0.5%–1% of price) are standard negotiation points.

### 3.3 Survival periods — the clock

How long the buyer can bring a claim after closing. Typical market carve-out structure:
- **Business/general warranties:** commonly **18–36 months** (long enough to get through at least one full audit cycle, so financial-statement issues surface).
- **Fundamental warranties (title/capacity):** much longer or unlimited.
- **Tax, employment, environmental, pensions:** typically tied to the **statute of limitations** for the underlying liability (these liabilities have long tails and are outside the buyer's control) — frequently the longest survival in the agreement.
- **Fraud:** unlimited.

> **The limitation package is a system.** Cap × survival × basket × de minimis together define the *real* recoverable envelope. Model it: a headline "50% cap" with a first-dollar basket and 36-month survival is far more protective than a "50% cap" with a deductible basket and 12-month survival. Quantify the effective coverage, don't read the cap in isolation.

### 3.4 Damages, mitigation, and exclusions

- **Duty to mitigate:** whether the buyer must take reasonable steps to reduce loss — varies by jurisdiction (in some it is a default legal duty; in others it must be contracted).
- **Exclusion of consequential/indirect damages:** commonly excluded — but "consequential" is notoriously ambiguous; sophisticated agreements *define* recoverable loss rather than relying on the label.
- **Buyer's-knowledge defences ("anti-sandbagging" vs. "pro-sandbagging"):** does the seller escape liability for a breach the buyer *knew about* at signing? **Pro-sandbagging** (buyer can still claim despite knowledge) is buyer-favourable and common in US practice; **anti-sandbagging** (knowledge bars the claim) is seller-favourable and the default in several other markets even when silent. This is a genuine cross-border divergence — never leave it to implication across jurisdictions.
- **Double-recovery bars:** no recovery to the extent a loss is already reflected in the accounts/price, recovered from a third party, or covered by insurance.

### 3.5 Set-offs and third-party recoveries

Whether recoveries the buyer makes elsewhere reduce the seller's liability — **insurance proceeds, third-party recoveries, and tax benefits (savings) arising from the loss** are commonly credited back to the seller (i.e., they reduce the claim). Each is negotiated; tax-benefit set-offs are the most contested because they are speculative.

## 4. Warranty & Indemnity (W&I) Insurance — The Market-Reshaper

W&I (rep-and-warranty) insurance has moved from exotic to mainstream and now *sets the terms* on a large share of mid-market and sponsor deals. It transfers warranty risk from the seller to an insurer, and in doing so **collapses the traditional seller-liability package**: caps drop to nominal, escrows shrink or vanish, and sellers achieve a "clean exit."

Key mechanics to understand:
- **Who buys it:** usually a *buy-side* policy — the buyer claims directly against the insurer for warranty breaches, preserving the seller relationship.
- **Retention/attachment:** the policy has a deductible (retention), often ~0.5%–1% of enterprise value, frequently split so the seller retains a small sliver of risk (a nominal cap, e.g., $1 or 1% of price) to keep incentives honest.
- **Coverage limits and exclusions:** insurers exclude known issues (matters raised in diligence), purchase-price adjustments, forward-looking statements, pensions underfunding, transfer pricing, and certain environmental/secondary-tax risks. **Known risks flushed by diligence don't disappear — they revert to specific indemnities or price.**
- **The diligence tie:** insurers require thorough diligence and will not cover gaps; the diligence report effectively underwrites the policy. This tightens, not loosens, diligence discipline.
- **Effect on negotiation:** with W&I in play, the buyer-seller fight over cap/survival cools (the insurer's terms govern), and energy shifts to *scope of warranties* (broader is better for an insured buyer), *disclosure standard*, and *the treatment of known/excluded risks.*

> **Consultant's read.** When W&I is present, don't benchmark the seller cap against uninsured deals — it will look artificially tiny and that's by design. Benchmark the *policy* (limit, retention, exclusions) and the *residual risks* pushed outside cover.

## 5. Covenants — Conduct Before and After Close

### 5.1 Pre-closing (interim) covenants
The seller runs the business in the **ordinary course** and refrains from listed value-affecting actions (dividends, capex above a threshold, new debt, material contract changes, key hires/fires, settlements) without buyer consent — *bounded by gun-jumping limits* (see `cross-border-ma-execution.md` §2.2/§3.4). **Broad access to books, records, and management between signing and closing** is commonly granted, subject to clean-team protocols for competitively sensitive information.

### 5.2 Restrictive covenants (non-compete / non-solicit)
Protecting the goodwill the buyer is paying for:
- **Non-compete on the seller:** common, and often essential to the value case where the seller could re-enter the market. Enforceability turns on *reasonableness* of duration, geography, and scope — courts in many jurisdictions will strike or narrow an overbroad covenant.
- **"Blue-pencil" / waterfall (cascading) drafting:** drafting the restriction in progressively narrower alternatives so a court can strike the widest and enforce a narrower one. Its *availability and effectiveness vary sharply by jurisdiction* — some courts blue-pencil freely, others refuse and void the entire covenant if any part is unreasonable. Know the local rule before relying on breadth.
- **Non-solicit of employees and of customers:** both common, generally easier to enforce than a full non-compete because less restrictive of trade.

## 6. Price Mechanics — Turning the Headline Number Into Cash

Two dominant mechanisms for setting the *actual* consideration, plus the security devices around it:

- **Completion accounts:** the price is adjusted *after* closing based on completion-date figures (net debt, working capital vs. a target/peg, sometimes net assets). Accurate but slow and dispute-prone; the working-capital peg is itself a negotiation.
- **Locked box:** the price is fixed by reference to a *historical* balance sheet (the "locked-box date"), with the seller barred from extracting value ("leakage") between that date and closing, save for agreed "permitted leakage." Faster, more certain, gives the seller price certainty and avoids post-closing disputes — increasingly the market default in competitive European-style processes; less dominant in some US practice.
- **Earn-outs:** deferring part of the price contingent on post-closing performance. Powerful for bridging valuation gaps, but a *dispute factory* — the buyer controls the business that generates the metric, so define the metric, the accounting, the operating covenants, and the dispute mechanism with extreme care.
- **Escrows / holdbacks:** a slice of consideration held by a third party to secure warranty/indemnity claims and price adjustments — the traditional security that W&I insurance increasingly replaces.

## 7. Governing Law, Dispute Resolution, and Boilerplate That Isn't

- **Choice of governing law:** most jurisdictions allow parties to choose the governing law of the SPA, and sophisticated cross-border deals frequently choose a *neutral, well-developed commercial law* (English or New York law are the common defaults) even where neither party is local — because of predictability and depth of precedent. Confirm the choice will be *respected* by the courts that matter (some mandatory local rules, e.g., on transfer formalities or competition, apply regardless of chosen law).
- **Arbitration vs. litigation:** cross-border deals frequently prefer **arbitration** (neutral forum, confidentiality, and — via the New York Convention — far easier cross-border *enforcement* of awards than of court judgments). Domestic deals more often use local courts. The choice turns on enforceability against the counterparty's assets, confidentiality needs, and speed.
- **Language, notarisation, and formality:** covered in `cross-border-ma-execution.md` §3.3 — but remember that *governing-law choice does not override local execution formalities.* An English-law SPA can still require a local notarial deed to transfer the shares.

## 8. The Deal-Terms Prioritisation Grid — Where to Spend Negotiating Capital

You cannot win every point, and trying signals inexperience. Prioritise by *materiality × contestedness*:

**Tier 1 — always fight (moves real money / kills the deal thesis):**
- Liability **cap** level and what it attaches to
- **Survival** periods for tax/environmental/employment (long-tail liabilities)
- **Basket type** (deductible vs. tipping) and threshold
- **MAC/MAE** definition and closing conditionality (the walk-away right)
- **Specific indemnities** for known, quantified diligence findings (these should *not* be diluted by general caps/baskets)
- **Price mechanic** (locked box vs. completion accounts) and the working-capital peg / leakage definition

**Tier 2 — fight when leverage allows:**
- **Knowledge standard** (actual vs. constructive; named individuals)
- **Materiality scrape** and anti-/pro-sandbagging
- **Data-room / general disclosure** standard
- **De minimis** level and **W&I** structure (who pays, retention, excluded risks)
- **Non-compete** scope and blue-pencil reliance

**Tier 3 — standardise, don't burn capital:**
- Boilerplate, notices, most procedural mechanics — *unless* a cross-border formality (governing law, arbitration, execution) makes them Tier 1 in disguise.

> **The through-line.** A private M&A agreement is a quantifiable risk system, not a wall of legalese. Identify the four or five dials that actually allocate money on *this* deal, benchmark each against market, model the *combined* effect of the limitation package, and concentrate negotiation there. Everything else is standardisable. That is how a commercial adviser adds value in the room alongside the lawyers — by keeping the negotiation anchored to *value at risk*, not clause count.
