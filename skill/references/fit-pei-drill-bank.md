# Fit & PEI Drill Bank — Question Universe, Model Skeletons & Self-Scoring

The **practice companion** to `behavioral-fit-interview.md`. That file teaches the *architecture* of a great fit answer (the story bank, STAR/PARADE/COIN, delivery standards); this one is the **drill ground** — a wide question universe to rehearse against, model story skeletons, worked treatments of the three anchor questions, and a self-score rubric. Distilled from public MBA casebook fit/PEI sections into native form.

Use it when: rehearsing behavioral answers, building or stress-testing a story bank, or coaching someone through "tell me about a time…". Read `behavioral-fit-interview.md` first for the frameworks; come here to drill.

---

## The fit interview is half the offer

The behavioral / personal-experience interview (PEI) is weighted as heavily as the case. It's where the interviewer decides whether they'd staff you on a stressed client at 11pm — testing **leadership, drive, impact, and personal insight**, not your résumé. Two broad question categories:

- **Professional / trajectory** — walk me through your résumé, why consulting, why this firm/office, career goals. Testing: clarity of motivation, judgment about your own path, communication.
- **Behavioral / character** — "tell me about a time…". Testing: leadership, influence, resilience, teaming, ethics — through evidence, not adjectives.

Both are scored the same way you score a case: structured, led with the headline, quantified, and concise.

## A delivery spine for stories: headline-first SOARL

`behavioral-fit-interview.md` gives five frameworks (STAR, START, CARL, PARADE, COIN). A useful sixth for the résumé-and-impact prompts is **headline-first**: open with a one-line *headline* of the outcome, then Situation → Obstacle → Action → Result → Learning. Leading with the result orients the interviewer (the pyramid principle, applied to your own story) and earns you the benefit of the doubt through the detail. Aim for **1–2.5 minutes**; the résumé walk-through and "why consulting/why firm" don't need the full arc.

```
HEADLINE  "I turned a failing student club into the largest on campus."
S  where it started (small, declining membership)
O  the real obstacle (no budget, apathetic committee)
A  what I SPECIFICALLY did (three moves)
R  quantified result (40 → 180 members, +$X funding)
L  what I now do differently because of it
```

## The three anchor questions — worked treatments

Prepare these three cold; almost every fit conversation opens on some mix of them.

**1. "Walk me through your résumé / tell me about yourself."** A through-line, not a recital. Start → 2–3 defining pivots (each with a quantified proof point) → why that path leads *here*. End on a hook that maps to what they want. ~90 seconds. *Weak:* chronological list of every role. *Strong:* "Three choices explain my path — each pushed me toward structured problem-solving, which is why consulting is the deliberate next step."

**2. "Why consulting / why this firm / why this office."** Three distinct halves, don't blur them. *Why consulting:* the specific skills/exposure you want (pace, breadth, structured problem-solving), tied to a concrete past experience that revealed the gap. *Why this firm:* practice strengths, culture, and **people you've actually spoken to** — specific, never a brochure paraphrase. *Why this office:* a real, personal reason (network, sector focus, geography). *Weak:* "you're the best firm." *Strong:* names a practice, a person, and a match to your trajectory.

**3. "Why you / why should we take you."** Your differentiated 2–3 strengths, each with a proof story from the bank. This is where the bank pays off — you're not asserting "I'm a leader," you're *selecting the story* that proves it for this prompt.

## Build the story bank first (6–10 stories → many questions)

Don't prep answer-by-answer — you'll be caught flat by novel phrasings. Build **6–10 richly-detailed real stories**, then map each to the competencies it can demonstrate. In the room you *select and angle* a story to the question. (Full mapping grid in `behavioral-fit-interview.md`.)

**Coverage test:** every competency column below should have **≥2 strong stories**. Gaps are where you get exposed.

## The question universe — drill against all of it

Rehearse out loud; map each to ≥1 bank story before you walk in.

### Professional / trajectory
- Walk me through your résumé. / Tell me who you are that isn't on the page.
- Tell me more about [specific bullet].
- Why consulting? What does consulting mean to you?
- Why this firm? Why this firm over the others? Why this office/city — what criteria?
- What are your short- and long-term goals? Where do you see yourself in 5–10 years?
- How would classmates/co-workers describe your leadership style?
- What distinguishes you from the other candidates I'm meeting today?
- What's the one thing you want me to remember about you?
- How would you evaluate the business of a past employer? (a mini-case in disguise)

### Leadership & influence
- A time you led a team. / led **without authority**. / motivated a disengaged member.
- A time you persuaded a team/manager/client of an idea they initially resisted.
- A time you led a team through significant change.
- A time you drove a decision you didn't control.

### Achievement & impact
- Your proudest accomplishment (not necessarily professional).
- A goal you set and hit. / A time you had a large impact on a team or project.
- A time you used analysis to guide a decision.

### Conflict & teaming
- A disagreement with your boss/team and how you handled it.
- A difficult teammate / a low-performing peer.
- A time you worked with people who approached things very differently.
- The worst team you've been on — why, and what you did.

### Failure & resilience
- Your biggest failure and what you learned. / A mistake and the lesson.
- A time you faced a significant challenge and how you overcame it.
- A weakness / professional challenge — and how you're working on it.
- Looking back, the one thing you'd do differently.

### Ambiguity & drive
- A time you operated under a tight deadline / with ambiguous data.
- A time you went beyond your remit. / juggled competing priorities.
- Your dream project.

### Ethics & values
- An ethical dilemma and how you navigated it.
- A time you pushed back on something you disagreed with.

## Delivery standards (same bar as the case)
- **"I," not "we."** State *your* specific contribution even inside a team story.
- **Quantify the result.** "40 → 180" beats "significantly increased." No number = no result.
- **Signpost.** "There were three things I did — first… second… third…" MECE applies here too.
- **Own the failure.** Pick a real one, take genuine accountability (no humble-brags), land the takeaway you now operate by.
- **Tailor, don't recite.** Angle the same bank story differently for "leadership" vs. "conflict." Reusing a story is fine; reusing the *emphasis* is the tell.
- **Expect follow-ups.** Interviewers drill in ("why did you do that? what would you change?"). Welcome it — depth is where you win. Interviewers from the same firm may compare notes, so keep it authentic and consistent.

## Self-scoring rubric (grade a practice answer 1–4)

| Dimension | 1 (weak) | 2 | 3 | 4 (strong) |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Structure** | Rambling, no arc | Loose STAR | Clear STAR, signposted | Headline-led, effortless arc |
| **Specificity** | Vague "we" | Some detail | Concrete "I" actions | Vivid, decision-level detail |
| **Impact** | No result | Qualitative result | Quantified result | Quantified + downstream effect |
| **Insight** | No reflection | Generic lesson | Real, owned lesson | Lesson visibly changed later behavior |
| **Fit to Q** | Answers a different question | Mostly on point | On point, angled | Precisely angled to the prompt |
| **Delivery** | Nervous, >3 min | Adequate | Composed, ~90s | Warm, conversational, invites follow-up |

**Coaching loop:** record the answer, score it, identify the lowest dimension, redo *only* that. Most improvement comes from moving Impact (quantify) and Insight (own the lesson) from 2 → 4.

## Context note — company-specific fit (India GM / leadership programmes)

For general-management and leadership-programme interviews at Indian corporate houses, fit skews **company-specific**: why this group, which business, how the role advances your path there. Prepare a per-target "why us" grounded in the group's value system and a recent development.
