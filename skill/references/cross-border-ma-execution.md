# Cross-Border M&A Execution — Structuring, Clearances, and Signing-to-Closing

> **When to read this.** You are advising on, diligencing, or modeling a private acquisition that crosses at least one border — or a domestic deal in an unfamiliar jurisdiction. This file is the *execution* layer that sits underneath valuation, synergy, and integration work. It answers: *How is the deal actually done here, what can stop it, and how long does it take?* For the negotiation of the purchase agreement itself, read `spa-deal-terms-benchmarking.md`. For diligence mechanics, read `due-diligence-deep-dive.md` and `data-room-speed-reading.md`. For after-close, read `post-merger-integration.md`.

Cross-border private M&A rewards people who treat *legal and regulatory execution as a workstream with its own critical path*, not as paperwork that follows the deal. Deals rarely die on price. They die on a foreign-investment veto, a merger-control Phase II, a failure to transfer a key contract or licence, a withholding-tax surprise, or a closing mechanic nobody sequenced. The elite operator builds the execution map on Day 1 alongside the valuation model.

---

## 1. The Universal Structure of a Private Deal (and Where Jurisdictions Diverge)

Every private acquisition, anywhere, answers the same five structural questions. What changes across borders is the *answer*, not the question. Carry this checklist into any new jurisdiction and you have working fluency in an afternoon.

1. **What are we buying — shares, assets, or a statutory merger?**
2. **Who must approve it — regulators, the state, shareholders, third parties?**
3. **How does title actually pass, and when?**
4. **What tax leaks on the way through?**
5. **What survives to bite us after close?**

### 1.1 Share purchase vs. asset purchase vs. statutory merger

These are the three acquisition methods, and the choice is the single most consequential structuring decision. It drives tax, liability, third-party consents, timeline, and deal-term negotiation.

**Share (or equity/quota) purchase.** The buyer acquires the ownership interests in the target entity, which continues to hold its assets, contracts, employees, and *liabilities* unchanged. This is the default for most going-concern deals.
- *Advantage:* clean and fast in principle — one thing changes hands (the shares); contracts and licences stay with the entity, so fewer third-party consents.
- *Disadvantage:* the buyer inherits the entity's history — every undisclosed liability, tax exposure, litigation, and environmental legacy. Diligence and indemnity protection therefore carry the entire weight of risk allocation.
- *Mechanics vary:* transferring legal title to shares can be as light as executing a short transfer form and updating the company's share register (common-law private companies), or as heavy as notarised deeds, registry filings, and statutory pre-emption waivers (many civil-law systems). In several jurisdictions the transfer of a *limited-liability company's* interests (quotas/participations) is materially more formal than transferring *stock-corporation* shares — the LLC transfer may require registry recording and even a by-law amendment, while the corporation's shares transfer by endorsement and register entry.

**Asset (or business) purchase.** The buyer cherry-picks specified assets and (usually) assumes only specified liabilities. Used when the buyer wants a clean perimeter, when the target is one division of a larger group (a carve-out), or when the seller entity carries risks the buyer refuses to inherit.
- *Advantage:* liability ring-fencing and a step-up in the tax basis of assets in many regimes (future depreciation shield).
- *Disadvantage:* *consent-heavy and slow.* Each material contract, lease, permit, and IP registration may need counterparty consent or re-issuance; employees typically do not transfer automatically (except where automatic-transfer employment regimes apply — see §5); and "bulk transfer" or "transfer of going concern" formalities may trigger creditor-notification waiting periods and successor-liability rules for tax and employment debts.

**Statutory (legal) merger.** Two entities combine by operation of law; one survives (absorption) or a new entity is formed (consolidation), and the disappearing entity's assets and liabilities pass by universal succession. Common in the US and most civil-law systems; **notably absent as a domestic concept in some common-law jurisdictions** (the UK has no general statutory merger of two companies; combinations are achieved by share/asset purchase or by a court-sanctioned scheme of arrangement). Cross-border statutory mergers exist in the EU/EEA framework but are used sparingly because of extended timetables and creditor-consent concerns.

> **Decision heuristic.** Start from the buyer's risk appetite and the location of value. *Value in the entity's contracts, licences, and network effects → lean share deal and price the inherited risk into indemnities.* *Value in discrete assets, or a toxic entity history, or a carve-out from a bigger group → lean asset deal and budget for consent time.* *Regulated, listed, or many-shareholder target → the statutory-merger or scheme route may be the only way to bind dissenters.*

### 1.2 The corporate-form lens

Before you can transfer anything you must know *what* you are transferring. Every jurisdiction offers a small menu of vehicles, and the two that matter for most private deals are the **stock corporation** (freely transferable shares, board governance, often "double taxation" of profits then dividends) and the **limited-liability company** (membership/quota interests, contractual flexibility, frequently transfer restrictions and formality). Read the target's constitutional documents *first* — they routinely contain pre-emption rights, transfer approvals, drag/tag provisions, and consent thresholds that dictate the mechanics and timeline of your deal before any regulator is involved.

Recurring form-driven traps:
- **Minimum shareholder/member counts and residency rules.** Some regimes require ≥2 holders, a resident director, or a local registered agent — relevant when structuring the acquisition vehicle.
- **Directors' liability bonds / guarantees.** A handful of jurisdictions require directors to lodge a bond on appointment — a friction when installing your own board at close.
- **Paid-in capital rules.** Where only part of subscribed cash capital must be paid on incorporation, an unpaid-capital call can be a hidden liability in a share deal.

## 2. The Clearance Map — What Can Stop the Deal

Two families of state control sit between signing and closing: **foreign-investment screening** (is *this buyer* allowed to own *this target*?) and **merger control / antitrust** (does the *combination* harm competition?). They are separate regimes, separately triggered, separately timed, and either can block or condition a deal. Map both on Day 1; each defines the deal's critical path and the allocation of regulatory risk in the agreement.

### 2.1 Foreign-investment (FDI) and national-security screening

Governments increasingly screen inbound acquisitions for national-security, critical-infrastructure, sensitive-technology, and strategic-sector concerns. The regimes differ in name and reach but share a common anatomy:

- **Sector triggers.** Defence, dual-use technology, critical infrastructure (energy, telecoms, ports, water), semiconductors and advanced computing, biotech, critical minerals, sensitive personal data, and media/broadcasting are near-universal sensitive sectors. Some regimes also restrict foreign ownership of domestic air and water transportation, banking, and insurance.
- **Trigger tests.** Acquisition of "control," crossing an ownership threshold (e.g., 10%/25%), or acquiring material influence — even a minority stake can trigger review in a sensitive sector.
- **Mandatory vs. voluntary filing.** Some regimes mandate pre-closing notification with standstill obligations and severe penalties (including unwinding a closed deal); others are voluntary but give the state a call-in power to review unnotified deals for years afterward — a latent risk that must be priced.
- **Remedies.** Approval subject to undertakings (security agreements, governance ring-fencing, data localisation, board/personnel restrictions, divestment of the sensitive unit) or outright prohibition.

*Illustrative regimes to name-check when scoping (verify current thresholds — these regimes change frequently):* the US **CFIUS** process (mandatory for certain critical-tech and sensitive-data/TID businesses; voluntary otherwise, with call-in risk); the UK **National Security and Investment Act** (mandatory notification across 17 sensitive sectors); the EU FDI Screening cooperation framework layered over divergent member-state regimes; and expanding regimes across Australia, Canada, Japan, and India (the last with an explicit prior-approval requirement for investment from bordering countries). **Do not assume a threshold from memory — pull the current rules for each relevant jurisdiction.**

> **Practitioner move.** Ask three questions early: *(1) Does the target do anything sensitive in any country where it operates? (2) Does the buyer's ultimate ownership include any state or "country-of-concern" links? (3) Is filing mandatory with a standstill, or voluntary with call-in risk?* The answers determine whether FDI is a footnote or the gating item.

### 2.2 Merger control / antitrust clearance

Merger control asks whether the combined entity would harm competition. Filings can be required in *every* jurisdiction where the parties have qualifying turnover or assets, so a single deal can face a dozen simultaneous reviews. The anatomy:

- **Jurisdictional thresholds.** Almost always turnover-based (combined and/or individual-party revenue in the jurisdiction), sometimes asset- or share-of-supply-based, and — increasingly — *transaction-value*-based to catch acquisitions of high-value, low-revenue targets (the "killer acquisition" concern in tech and pharma).
- **Suspensory ("stand-still") obligation.** Most regimes prohibit closing before clearance; "gun-jumping" (closing early or coordinating competitively sensitive conduct pre-clearance) draws heavy fines.
- **Two-phase structure.** A short Phase I review clears the large majority of deals; problematic deals go to an in-depth Phase II with a long clock.
- **Remedies.** Structural (divestiture of overlapping businesses) or behavioural (access, interoperability, pricing commitments). Structural remedies are generally preferred by authorities as cleaner to monitor.
- **Enforcement teeth.** Fines for failure to notify or for supplying misleading information can reach a percentage of the parties' worldwide turnover; authorities can compel information, conduct unannounced inspections ("dawn raids"), and unwind consummated transactions.

*Illustrative benchmark — the EU regime (verify current figures):* a "concentration" has an EU dimension, and must be notified to the European Commission before implementation, where the parties have combined worldwide turnover above **€5 billion** and EU-wide turnover of each of at least two parties above **€250 million** — unless each achieves more than **two-thirds** of its EU turnover in one and the same member state (the "two-thirds rule," which sends the deal to national authorities instead). A one-stop-shop principle means an EU-dimension filing generally ousts parallel national filings within the EEA. Phase I runs ~25 working days (extendable); Phase II adds ~90+ working days. Fines for gun-jumping or misleading information can reach 10% of aggregate turnover. Treat these numbers as a *shape*, not a current citation.

**Multi-jurisdiction sequencing.** On a global deal, identify the "long-pole" jurisdictions (those with the slowest clocks or the substantive overlap concerns) and drive the overall timeline off them. Coordinate filings so that arguments and remedies offered in one forum do not undercut another. The regulatory-conditions clause in the SPA must define exactly which clearances are conditions to closing, who bears the risk and cost of obtaining them ("hell-or-high-water" vs. "reasonable best efforts" vs. capped divestiture obligations), and what happens if a clearance is refused (break fee, long-stop date, walk-away right).

### 2.3 Other pre-closing approvals to inventory

- **Sector regulators:** banking, insurance, financial-services, telecoms, energy, healthcare, and aviation regulators routinely require change-of-control approval independent of antitrust.
- **Third-party contractual consents:** change-of-control clauses in key customer, supplier, financing, lease, IP-licence, and JV agreements. Diligence these early — a single anchor-customer consent can be the real critical path.
- **Works-council / employee consultation:** in employee-protective jurisdictions, information-and-consultation obligations must complete before signing or closing (see §5).
- **Exchange-control / central-bank clearance:** in some emerging markets, moving purchase consideration across the border requires prior approval or registration, without which the seller cannot repatriate proceeds.

## 3. The Deal Timeline — From LOI to Post-Closing

A disciplined execution map has five phases. Depths and names vary; the sequence does not.

### 3.1 Pre-contractual phase: LOI, exclusivity, and the duty of good faith

The letter of intent / term sheet fixes headline economics and process. Most of it is non-binding, but the binding provisions — **exclusivity/no-shop, confidentiality, cost allocation, and governing law** — matter enormously. A critical cross-border trap: the **legal weight of pre-contractual conduct differs by legal tradition.** Many civil-law systems impose a duty of good faith in negotiations (*culpa in contrahendo*): abruptly or arbitrarily breaking off advanced negotiations can create liability for the counterparty's wasted costs, even absent a signed deal. Common-law systems generally allow parties to walk away until signing, subject to any express binding LOI terms. Advise clients to *say less, in writing, earlier* in good-faith jurisdictions, and to make non-binding intent explicit.

### 3.2 Diligence and structuring (run in parallel, not series)

Legal, financial, tax, commercial, operational, ESG, and IT diligence run concurrently with structuring. The structuring output is a **step plan**: the sequence of entity formations, financings, transfers, and elections that gets the buyer from here to a clean ownership position at the lowest friction and tax cost. Tax structuring (§4) frequently *reshapes* the deal — turning a one-step share purchase into a multi-step reorganisation to preserve losses, achieve an asset step-up, or route consideration efficiently.

### 3.3 Signing

The parties execute the definitive agreement (SPA/APA/merger agreement) plus its schedules, disclosure letter, and ancillary documents (see the closing-document set in §6). **Execution formalities vary and are not a formality.** Some jurisdictions require *notarisation* of share transfers or of the whole agreement (common in civil-law systems for the transfer of LLC interests and real estate), *apostille/legalisation* of foreign corporate authority documents, wet-ink originals, or specific language versions. A deal can be economically agreed and legally void because a signature block was executed the wrong way. Confirm the execution mechanics *weeks* before signing, especially where cross-border powers of attorney and notaries must be scheduled.

### 3.4 The interim (signing-to-closing) period

Deals with conditions precedent — regulatory clearances, third-party consents, financing — have a gap between signing and closing that can run weeks to a year. This period is governed by:
- **Conditions precedent (CPs):** the exhaustive list of what must be satisfied or waived before either party is obliged to close. Categorise them as *regulatory*, *third-party consent*, *no-MAC / bring-down*, *financing* (increasingly rare as a condition on the buyer side in competitive processes), and *deliverables*.
- **Interim (pre-closing) covenants:** the seller must operate the business in the ordinary course and refrain from a defined list of value-affecting actions (dividends, capex, new debt, key-hires/fires, contract changes) without buyer consent — carefully bounded because *coordinating too closely can itself be gun-jumping* under merger control. Set up a **clean team / clean room** to exchange competitively sensitive information without antitrust exposure.
- **The long-stop (drop-dead) date:** the date after which either party can walk if CPs are unmet — the pressure valve that forces resolution and allocates the risk of regulatory delay.

### 3.5 Closing and post-closing

At closing, CPs are confirmed satisfied, the transfer instruments are executed, consideration is paid (often via escrow), and control passes. Post-closing steps include **completion-account or locked-box price adjustments** (see `spa-deal-terms-benchmarking.md`), registry filings to perfect title, notifications, and the start of the integration and warranty-survival clocks. Cross-border closings are frequently *virtual and choreographed* across time zones with a signed closing agenda and a "closing memorandum" confirming each step occurred in the required order — because in some jurisdictions title only passes on a registry act that must happen in local business hours.

## 4. Tax and Consideration Structuring — The Silent Value Driver

Tax structuring routinely moves more value than a full round of price negotiation, and it is where cross-border deals are won or lost quietly.

- **Share vs. asset, revisited through tax.** Buyers usually prefer assets for the *step-up* (higher depreciable basis → future tax shield) and liability ring-fencing; sellers usually prefer shares for a single capital-gains event and to avoid a two-layer tax (corporate-level gain on asset sale, then shareholder-level tax on distribution). The gap between these preferences is a negotiable value pool — sometimes bridged by a purchase-price adjustment or a joint tax election that gives the buyer a step-up while limiting the seller's downside.
- **Transfer taxes and duties.** Share transfers can attract stamp duty or financial-transaction tax (e.g., a stamp duty charged as a small percentage of consideration on share transfers in some common-law jurisdictions); asset transfers can attract VAT/GST (often relieved where the transfer qualifies as a "transfer of a going concern"), real-estate transfer tax, and registration fees. These are real leakage — model them.
- **Withholding tax and treaty planning.** Cross-border flows of purchase consideration, dividends, interest, and royalties can suffer withholding. The acquisition structure — including where the acquisition vehicle and any holding companies sit — is often designed around double-tax treaties and anti-abuse rules (principal-purpose tests, limitation-on-benefits, controlled-foreign-company and interest-deductibility limitation regimes, and the global minimum-tax framework). *Do not design a holding structure on treaty rates from memory; these are among the fastest-changing rules in tax.*
- **Losses, elections, and reorganisation reliefs.** Preserving the target's tax losses through a change of control, qualifying for tax-neutral reorganisation reliefs, and timing elections are frequent drivers of the step plan.
- **Repatriation and exchange control.** In some emerging markets, the ability of a foreign seller to take proceeds out of the country depends on prior registration of the original foreign investment — a diligence item that can trap the *seller's* value and become the buyer's leverage.

> Bring tax counsel into structuring *before* the SPA is drafted. Retrofitting tax onto an agreed structure is where value and goodwill are destroyed.

## 5. People, Assets, and Consents That Don't Move Automatically

Even a "clean" share deal, and especially an asset deal, runs into things that resist transfer.

- **Automatic-transfer employment regimes.** Many jurisdictions protect employees on a *business/asset transfer* by moving them automatically to the buyer on existing terms, with information-and-consultation obligations and restrictions on transfer-related dismissals. This converts an asset deal's "we'll pick the employees we want" assumption into "we inherit the workforce and must consult first." Identify these regimes early — they reshape both timeline (consultation before signing/closing) and economics (redundancy and harmonisation costs).
- **Works councils and unions.** Employee-representative bodies may have information, consultation, or (rarely) consent rights that gate signing. Budget calendar time; a rushed or skipped consultation can invalidate steps or trigger penalties and reputational damage.
- **Contracts with change-of-control or anti-assignment clauses.** Key customer, supplier, financing, lease, franchise, and IP-licence agreements frequently allow the counterparty to terminate or renegotiate on a change of control (share deal) or require consent to assignment (asset deal). Diligence these for the *crown-jewel* contracts first; a lost anchor contract can vaporise the deal thesis.
- **Permits, licences, and registrations.** Operating licences, environmental permits, and IP registrations may not travel with an asset deal and can require re-application or regulator consent even in a share deal.
- **Pensions and benefits.** Defined-benefit scheme deficits, unfunded liabilities, and benefit-harmonisation costs are classic hidden liabilities — diligence and price them explicitly.

## 6. The Closing-Document Set (Orientation)

You do not need to draft these, but you must know they exist, what each does, and which sit on the critical path. A typical private cross-border deal assembles:

- **Definitive agreement** — SPA / APA / merger agreement (economics, reps & warranties, covenants, indemnities, conditions, termination — see `spa-deal-terms-benchmarking.md`).
- **Disclosure letter / schedules** — the seller's qualifications against the warranties; the true risk map of the target.
- **Ancillary transfer instruments** — share transfer forms/deeds, assignment and assumption agreements, IP and real-estate transfers, each with its own execution formality.
- **Ancillary commercial agreements** — transitional services agreement (TSA), reverse-TSA, supply/manufacturing, IP-licence-back, and shareholders'/JV agreement where the seller rolls over.
- **Financing documents** — debt commitment and definitive facility documents; equity commitment letters.
- **Corporate authority** — board and shareholder resolutions, powers of attorney, secretary's/officer's certificates, notarial and apostille/legalisation documents.
- **Escrow / paying-agent arrangements** — for holdbacks, price-adjustment buffers, and indemnity security.
- **Closing agenda and closing memorandum** — the choreography and the proof it was followed.

## 7. The 12-Point Cross-Border Execution Diagnostic

Run this on any cross-border private deal in the first 48 hours. Each "unknown" is a workstream to open, not a box to leave blank.

1. **Method:** share, asset, or statutory merger — and *why*, tied to where value and risk sit?
2. **Vehicle & constitution:** target's corporate form, and what its charter/by-laws say about transfer, pre-emption, and consent.
3. **FDI screening:** any sensitive activity, buyer ownership sensitivity, mandatory-vs-call-in — in *every* operating country?
4. **Merger control:** in which jurisdictions are filing thresholds met; where is the substantive overlap; who bears clearance risk?
5. **Sector regulators:** any change-of-control approvals (banking, insurance, telecoms, energy, health, aviation)?
6. **Third-party consents:** which crown-jewel contracts, leases, licences, and financings have change-of-control/assignment triggers?
7. **Employees:** automatic-transfer regime? works-council/union consultation? redundancy and harmonisation cost?
8. **Tax:** transfer taxes, VAT/GST, withholding, treaty routing, loss preservation, step-up availability?
9. **Exchange control / repatriation:** any central-bank approval or investment-registration gating consideration flow?
10. **Execution formalities:** notarisation, apostille/legalisation, language, wet-ink, POAs — scheduled and feasible by signing?
11. **Interim-period risk:** long-stop date, clean-team protocol, ordinary-course covenants, MAC scope, gun-jumping guardrails?
12. **Survival:** what liabilities (tax, employment, environmental, litigation) outlive close, and how are they secured (escrow, W&I, specific indemnities)?

> **The through-line.** In domestic deals you can sometimes let the lawyers "handle closing." Across borders you cannot: the clearance map, tax step plan, consent inventory, and execution formalities are the deal's real critical path, and the value case is only as good as the ability to actually close, transfer title, and repatriate proceeds. Build the execution map on Day 1 and revisit it at every deal checkpoint.
