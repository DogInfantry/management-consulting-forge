# Case Bank — Interview Classics (Practice Menu + Worked Exemplars)

A curated bank of the **classic interview-case archetypes** that recur across published MBA casebooks and firm prep materials — re-engineered into native structure and rewritten in this skill's own voice. This is the *interview-flavoured* companion to `case-bank-worked.md` (compact sector drills) and `practice-cases-quantified.md` (a few full-length, deeply worked cases). Where those emphasise real-world business texture, this file preserves the **feel of a live interview case** — the evocative one-line prompt, the firm's house style, the single exhibit, the "so what" at the end.

**Sourcing & taste.** Case names and sectors are kept deliberately vivid (Army Hotel, Burger Palace, Elena's Electronics, Knight Perfumes) because that flavour is the point — a bland "Company X in Industry Y" teaches nothing about reading a real prompt. Firm-style tags (McKinsey-style, BCG-style, Bain-style, etc.) are kept as *descriptors of interview format and difficulty*, not claims of affiliation. All figures are **[ILLUSTRATIVE]** — demonstrative anchors, not real data. Nothing here is proprietary firm IP; these are widely-circulated public archetypes distilled into original worked form.

**How to use.** As a candidate: read only the **Prompt**, structure it cold, then check yourself against **Structure → Spine → Aha → Trap**. As an interviewer/coach: give only the Prompt, release each data point when the candidate asks for the right node, and grade against `case-facilitation-and-scoring.md`. Reason *from* these patterns — don't recite them.

Pairs with: `case-types.md` (framework selection), `case-pattern-library.md` (archetypes), `case-cracking-drills.md` (math, exhibit, structuring drills), `case-facilitation-and-scoring.md` (interviewer mode + rubric), `structured-case-approach.md` (the "isolate before you explain" move).

## Worked Exemplars

Fourteen fully cracked cases spanning every type above. Format: **Prompt** (what the candidate hears) → **Clarify** (the 2–3 questions that actually matter) → **Structure** (the tailored tree) → **Spine** (the quantified/logical crack) → **Aha** (the insight that closes it) → **Trap** (what sinks most candidates). Numbers are **[ILLUSTRATIVE]**.

### 1. Army Hotel — Market Entry (Hospitality, McKinsey-style, ●●)
**Prompt.** A private-equity firm can win a concession to build and run hotels on military bases, serving families visiting enlisted personnel. Should they take it?
**Clarify.** What's the investor's return bar and horizon? Is demand (visitor volume) contracted or open-market? Who bears the build cost — us or the base?
**Structure.** Two-fold investor lens: (1) **standalone attractiveness** — market size × our capturable share × unit economics; (2) **fit & risk** — concession terms, exit, base-closure risk. Entry-profitability tree: `Revenue (rooms × occupancy × ADR) − Cost (build/amortised + run) vs. hurdle IRR`.
**Spine.** Demand is unusually *predictable and captive* — visitors come for a fixed reason to a fixed place with few substitutes near base. Anchor occupancy off enlisted headcount × visit frequency × length of stay; benchmark ADR to a mid-tier chain (e.g. a Hampton Inn at ~$75/night [ILLUSTRATIVE]). The swing variable is **concession length vs. payback period** — a 10-year concession against a 12-year payback kills it regardless of demand.
**Aha.** A captive, low-substitute demand pool makes revenue forecastable; the deal lives or dies on **concession terms and exit**, not on demand risk.
**Trap.** Sizing the market beautifully, then never pressure-testing whether the concession outlasts the payback.

### 2. Burger Palace — Market Entry (QSR, candidate-led, ●●)
**Prompt.** A domestic burger chain wants to enter a new country. Go or no-go?
**Clarify.** Objective — profit, footprint, or strategic beachhead? Entry mode open (own vs. franchise vs. JV)? Time horizon?
**Structure.** 3C-into-entry: **Country/Customer** (market size, taste fit, income, existing QSR penetration) · **Competition** (incumbents, local chains, real-estate scarcity) · **Company** (right to win — brand, supply chain, capital) → **Entry mode & economics**.
**Spine.** Market = eating-out occasions × QSR share × burger share × spend × our capturable share. The decisive node is usually **local taste/format fit and supply chain**, not raw market size — a huge market you can't serve profitably (cold chain, local beef norms) is a no.
**Aha.** Entry is a *right-to-win* question, not a *market-size* question; a smaller market you can dominate beats a giant one you can only rent share in.
**Trap.** Declaring "big market → enter" without an entry-mode and unit-economics check.

### 3. Contact Lenses — Profitability (Consumer products, McKinsey-style, ●●)
**Prompt.** A contact-lens maker's profits are down. Diagnose and fix.
**Clarify.** Revenue or cost driven? Which product line/channel? Industry down too, or just us?
**Structure.** `Profit = Σ(price − unit cost) × volume` by **product** (dailies vs. monthlies) and **channel** (optometrist vs. retail vs. online). Isolate before explaining.
**Spine.** Split the decline: if industry is flat and we're down, it's **share/company-specific**. Dailies carry higher price but higher COGS/unit; a mix-shift toward a low-contribution SKU or a margin-thin online channel can erode blended margin with revenue flat.
**Aha.** "Profit down" with flat revenue is a **mix or cost** story — decompose contribution by SKU and channel before touching price.
**Trap.** Jumping to "raise prices" before locating whether the leak is price, cost, or mix.

### 4. Knight Perfumes — Profitability (CPG, interviewer-led, ●●)
**Prompt.** A men's-fragrance brand (deodorant spray, mild perfume, strong perfume; US; online + quick-commerce) has seen profit fall this past year, across all three lines by volume. Find why and restore it.
**Clarify.** Two-fold objective (why + how to fix)? All SKUs falling on *volume* (given)? Channels — pure e-comm/q-comm (given)?
**Structure.** All lines down on volume ⇒ a **demand or availability** problem, not one bad SKU. Tree: **Demand** (category trend, our share, brand/marketing, price vs. substitutes) × **Availability/Channel** (platform visibility, stock-outs, rising q-commerce take-rates squeezing margin) × **Cost** (input, fulfilment).
**Spine.** Volume falling across the board with an online-only footprint points at **channel economics or discoverability**: a q-commerce platform raising commissions or de-ranking the brand hits all SKUs at once. Size margin bridge: contribution/unit × Δvolume + Δtake-rate × revenue.
**Aha.** Uniform volume decline across SKUs is rarely product-level — look one layer up at **category demand or the platform gate** every SKU shares.
**Trap.** Building a per-SKU relaunch plan when the shared cause is a channel/discoverability shift.

### 5. Race to the Bottom — Profitability (Airlines, candidate-led, ●●, case-math heavy)
**Prompt.** A carrier's profit is collapsing amid a fare war. What's happening and what do we do?
**Clarify.** Which routes/segments? Load factor vs. yield moving? Cost structure fixed vs. variable?
**Structure.** Airline profit = `(RASK − CASK) × ASK`. Decompose: **yield** (fare × load) vs. **unit cost** (fuel, labour, ownership). In a fare war, yield falls; the question is whether marginal routes still beat **variable** cost.
**Spine.** With mostly fixed costs, the trap is matching every fare cut. Keep any route where price ≥ variable cost/seat (it contributes to fixed overhead); cut routes below it. Quantify contribution per seat-mile before pruning.
**Aha.** In a high-fixed-cost business, the decision rule is **contribution vs. variable cost**, not profit vs. fully-loaded cost — you fly a "loss-making" route if it still covers its variable cost.
**Trap.** Blanket fare-matching or blanket route cuts instead of a contribution-margin scalpel.

### 6. Cleaning Products — Growth (Consumer products, McKinsey-style, ●)
**Prompt.** A household-cleaning maker wants a 3–5 year growth strategy. Where should growth come from?
**Clarify.** Growth of revenue or profit? Organic vs. inorganic on the table? Any capability/geographic constraints?
**Structure.** Growth tree: **Market analysis** (size, growth, drivers) → **Organic** (existing products: price/volume; new products; new segments/channels/geos) → **Inorganic** (M&A, synergies, fit). Prioritise by attractiveness × right-to-win.
**Spine.** Rank the boxes: adjacent segments/channels where the brand already has permission beat white-space bets. Size the top 2 levers (e.g. new eco-line into existing retail × penetration × price) before recommending.
**Aha.** Growth strategy is a **prioritised portfolio of moves**, not a single idea — lead with the 20% of levers carrying 80% of the upside.
**Trap.** Listing every Ansoff box without sizing or sequencing them.

### 7. Coffee and Tea Apparel — M&A (Retail/Apparel, Deloitte-style, ●●)
**Prompt.** A coffee-and-tea retailer is considering merging with an apparel brand. Should it?
**Clarify.** Strategic rationale — growth, cost synergy, diversification? Return bar? Cultural/brand fit concern?
**Structure.** M&A tree: **Why** (strategic logic) · **Target attractiveness** (standalone economics) · **Synergies** (revenue: cross-sell, channel; cost: shared retail/back-office; dis-synergies) · **Deal** (price, integration risk, alternatives) → NPV vs. price.
**Spine.** The revenue-synergy story (sell apparel to coffee footfall) is usually **over-claimed** — different purchase occasion, different customer intent. Stress cross-sell attach rate hard; most value, if any, is in **shared real estate/back-office cost**.
**Aha.** Revenue synergies in unrelated-adjacency merges are the first thing to distrust; **prove the cross-sell** before it earns a place in the model.
**Trap.** Accepting management's synergy number without a reality check on customer overlap.

### 8. Cruise Line Acquisition — M&A / Investment (Travel, BCG-style, ●●●)
**Prompt.** A PE fund is weighing the purchase of a cruise operator. Attractive at the asking price?
**Clarify.** Entry multiple & hold period? Fleet age/capex needs? Cyclicality/where in the cycle?
**Structure.** LBO-lite lens: **Market** (demand growth, cyclicality, seasonality) · **Asset** (fleet age, maintenance capex, occupancy/yield) · **Deal** (leverage, entry/exit multiple, cash generation) → IRR/MOIC.
**Spine.** Cruise economics hinge on **occupancy × onboard spend × yield** against a huge fixed asset with heavy maintenance capex. Model free cash after dry-dock capex; test IRR to occupancy and fuel. Cyclicality means **entry timing** dominates.
**Aha.** For a capital-heavy cyclical, returns are made on **buy price and maintenance-capex discipline**, not on optimistic yield growth.
**Trap.** Modelling smooth yield growth and ignoring the lumpy dry-dock capex that eats the cash flow.

### 9. New Vaccine — Pricing (Pharma, LEK-style, ●●)
**Prompt.** A firm is launching a novel vaccine in the US. What price?
**Clarify.** Objective — profit, access, share? Competing prophylaxis? Payer/reimbursement context?
**Structure.** Pricing triangulation: **Cost floor** (R&D-inclusive, unit) · **Competitive reference** (existing prophylaxis/standard of care) · **Value ceiling** (economic value = downstream treatment cost avoided × efficacy). Then payer & volume.
**Spine.** Value-based ceiling = cost-of-illness avoided (hospitalisations × cost × cases prevented). Price between reference and value, adjusted for payer willingness and access goals. Volume × (price − unit cost) − launch cost.
**Aha.** Novel therapy pricing is anchored to **economic value created (illness avoided)**, not cost-plus — the ceiling is what the health system saves.
**Trap.** Cost-plus pricing that leaves most of the created value on the table, or a price payers won't reimburse.

### 10. Lost Patent — Revenue Defence / Pricing (Pharma, ATK-style, ●●●)
**Prompt.** A blockbuster drug is about to lose patent protection. How do we defend revenue?
**Clarify.** Time to LOE? Share of portfolio? Generic-entry expectation (how many entrants)?
**Structure.** Post-LOE revenue = branded-retained + adjacent recapture. Levers: **product** (authorised generic, next-gen reformulation, OTC switch), **channel/patient** (loyalty, bundling), **portfolio** (pipeline replacement). Time-box each.
**Spine.** Generics typically take the majority of volume within a year at a fraction of price [ILLUSTRATIVE], so defending the *molecule's* revenue is largely futile — value shifts to **authorised generic capture + next-gen switch + replacing the revenue from pipeline**.
**Aha.** After LOE you don't defend the old revenue line — you **manage the decline and shift the base** to an authorised generic and the next asset.
**Trap.** Pouring marketing into a branded product whose price advantage legally evaporated.

### 11. White Boards — Make vs. Buy / Sourcing (Durable goods, Bain-style, ●●)
**Prompt.** A maker of a durable good is deciding whether to keep producing a key component in-house or outsource it. Which?
**Clarify.** Objective — cost, quality, flexibility, strategic control? Volume & utilisation of the in-house line? Supplier landscape?
**Structure.** Total-cost-to-serve comparison: **In-house** (variable + allocated fixed + capex/D&A + quality/flex) vs. **Outsource** (unit price + logistics + coordination + IP/strategic risk). Plus strategic: is this component a source of differentiation?
**Spine.** Compare **relevant** costs only: if outsourcing, the in-house *fixed* cost may not disappear (stranded assets) — use avoidable cost, not fully-loaded. If the component is core to differentiation, weight strategic control above a small unit-cost gap.
**Aha.** Make-vs-buy turns on **avoidable cost + strategic control**, not headline unit price — stranded fixed costs and lost differentiation sink naïve outsourcing.
**Trap.** Comparing outsource price to fully-loaded in-house cost and ignoring which costs actually go away.

### 12. Heavy Attrition — Operations / Org (Healthcare, ZS-style, ●)
**Prompt.** A healthcare provider is bleeding frontline staff. Stop the attrition.
**Clarify.** Which roles/sites? Voluntary vs. involuntary? Benchmark vs. sector turnover?
**Structure.** Attrition tree: **why they leave** (pay, workload/burnout, management, progression, alternatives) × **where** (role/site concentration). Diagnose drivers → size cost of attrition (replacement + productivity loss) → targeted interventions.
**Spine.** Quantify the *cost* of a leaver (recruit + onboard + ramp + overtime backfill) to justify spend, then attack the top 1–2 drivers where turnover concentrates. Often it's **workload + progression**, not headline pay.
**Aha.** Attrition is an **economic** problem — size the cost of a leaver first; that reframes retention spend from "soft cost" to hard ROI.
**Trap.** A blanket pay rise when the driver is burnout/scheduling at two specific sites.

### 13. Avalon Education Policy — Non-traditional / Public (Public sector, interviewer-led, ●●)
**Prompt.** A national government wants a new 10-year education policy across three goals — **access, quality, accountability**. What initiatives should it include?
**Clarify.** Targets per goal (e.g. access gap ~80% of schools; quality ≥ "B" on an index; ≥90% graduate employability)? Budget/constraints? Short- vs. long-term mix?
**Structure.** Structure *by the client's own goals* — Access / Quality / Accountability — each with initiatives split **short-term (quick wins)** vs. **structural (10-year)**. This is a design case: MECE the solution space, don't diagnose a problem.
**Spine.** Under each goal, generate initiatives and prioritise by impact × feasibility × cost. Access: infrastructure, transport, subsidies, digital. Quality: teacher training, curriculum, standards. Accountability: assessment, data systems, outcome-linked funding. Sequence and cost the top few.
**Aha.** For a **design/"what should we do"** case, the client's stated objectives *are* your top-level structure; the value is in prioritised, sequenced, costed initiatives — not a diagnosis.
**Trap.** Treating a design prompt like a diagnostic one, or brainstorming an unstructured list with no prioritisation.

### 14. Jurassic Vale — Market Sizing + Investment (Entertainment, candidate-led, ●●)
**Prompt.** An investor is considering a novelty theme-park concept. Size the opportunity and advise.
**Clarify.** Region/catchment? Ticket-price assumption? One-off novelty or repeat-visit model?
**Structure.** Size demand bottom-up: **catchment population × visit propensity × frequency × capture**, plus tourists. Then investment lens: `Revenue (tickets + F&B + merch) − (build capex amortised + opex) vs. hurdle`.
**Spine.** Bottom-up annual visitors = addressable population (within X-hour drive) × % who visit/year × our share + inbound tourists. Revenue/visitor = ticket + ancillary. Test payback of the build capex against attendance; novelty concepts risk a **decay curve** after year one.
**Aha.** Size **bottom-up from the catchment**, and for a novelty attraction, explicitly model **attendance decay** — year-one crowds don't annuitise.
**Trap.** Top-down "1% of the country" sizing, and assuming flat attendance for a novelty product.

## Building your own crack fast

For any interview classic, the 60–90 second move is: (1) restate the **objective** and success metric, (2) pick the **type** (`case-types.md`) and lay a *tailored* MECE tree — "revenue from X" beats "revenue", (3) state a **Day-1 hypothesis** and the branch you'd test first, (4) signal the **quant** you'd run and the **exhibit** you'd want. Then drive. The exemplars above show the shape; the drills in `case-cracking-drills.md` build the reflexes; `case-facilitation-and-scoring.md` grades the result.
